What a Dean Sees

This view is designed to answer one question in under 10 seconds: "Are our students meeting our outcomes, and where do we need to act?"

No charts. No statistics. Sun, clouds, rain. Every card is one sentence of substance — not "3.2 out of 4" but "84% of seniors demonstrate proficiency, up 7% from last cycle."

The person who needs the full heatmap can click through. The person who needs to report to the board can screenshot this page.

Concept: The narrative evidence report — what actually goes to the accreditor. Not a spreadsheet of scores, but a compelling, data-backed narrative with real student evidence. Generated from everything in the system.
Accreditation Evidence Report
Student Outcome Assessment Report
Mechanical Engineering Program · ABET Criterion 3, Outcome 1
Assessment Period: Fall 2024 – Spring 2026

Outcome 1 — Problem Analysis: "An ability to identify, formulate, and solve complex engineering problems by applying principles of engineering, science, and mathematics."

Finding: 84% of graduating seniors (71 of 84 students) demonstrate proficiency in problem analysis, exceeding our threshold of 70%. This represents a 7-point improvement from the previous assessment cycle (77% in 2022–2024).

Evidence Sources:

1. Direct Assessment (Rubric): In the Senior Design capstone (ME 495), the Problem Formulation criterion of the course rubric showed 84% of students at "Proficient" or "Advanced" levels. N=84.

2. Reflective Practice Analysis (Tori): AI-assisted analysis of 2,847 student reflective comments across 6 courses revealed that 91% of engineering students engaged with Cognitive-Analytical learning dimensions. The Problem-Solving indicator appeared in 76% of student conversations, with 62% of those reaching Dialogic or Critical reflection depth — suggesting not just application but genuine metacognitive engagement with problem-solving processes.

3. Portfolio Evidence: 23 of 28 sampled capstone portfolios (82%) included problem analysis artifacts with faculty annotations confirming outcome achievement.

4. Longitudinal Growth: Tracking students from their first engineering course to capstone, we observe consistent deepening of analytical engagement. The cohort's "Problem Analysis signature" — a multi-dimensional profile of how students approach problems — shows progressive complexity: first-year students rely primarily on procedural approaches, while seniors demonstrate evaluation, synthesis, and metacognitive monitoring of their problem-solving strategies.

Student example: "I realized my initial model was oversimplified — I was treating the thermal load as uniform when the actual distribution follows a gradient that changes with the boundary conditions. Going back to re-derive the equations with the actual boundary conditions gave me a completely different result, and that's when I understood why the simulation wasn't matching our experimental data." — ME 495 student, Spring 2026

Growth Trajectory: The Class of 2026 showed stronger growth than the Class of 2025, with evidence of deepening particularly between Years 2 and 3, coinciding with the introduction of reflective practice components in ME 300 and ME 350.

Action Items: While overall achievement is strong, 16% of students (13 individuals) did not reach proficiency. Analysis of their Tori profiles suggests these students engaged primarily at the Descriptive level — describing procedures without analyzing why they work. The department will pilot targeted metacognitive scaffolding in ME 300 (Fall 2026) to address this gap earlier in the curriculum.
