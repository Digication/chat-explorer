Thermal Load Analysis in Sustainable Building Design
ME 300 · Research Paper · Draft 2 · Last saved 2 min ago
1. Introduction
This paper examines thermal load distribution in sustainable building envelopes, with particular attention to how boundary conditions affect energy modeling accuracy. As green building standards become more stringent, the gap between simulated and actual thermal performance has become a critical concern for designers and engineers. Reflected
Previous approaches have relied on uniform thermal load assumptions for computational simplicity. While this reduces modeling complexity, it may introduce systematic errors that compound across building zones, particularly in mixed-use structures where internal heat generation varies significantly by space function.
2. Literature Review
Chen et al. (2023) demonstrated that spatially resolved thermal models produced predictions within 5% of measured values for commercial buildings, compared to 12-18% error with uniform assumptions. Their work focused primarily on cooling-dominated climates, leaving open the question of whether similar improvements apply to heating-dominated regions.
Conversely, Park and Williams (2024) argued that the computational cost of fully resolved models outweighs the accuracy gains for preliminary design stages, advocating for a tiered approach where simplified models guide early decisions and detailed models validate final designs. This tension between accuracy and practicality is central to the present study.
3. Methodology
The study employed a dual-modeling approach: a baseline model using uniform thermal loads (following ASHRAE Standard 140) and an enhanced model incorporating spatially varying boundary conditions derived from field measurements.
Both models were applied to a three-story mixed-use building in Providence, RI, with monitored thermal data collected over a 14-month period. Temperature sensors were placed at 0.5m intervals along exterior walls at each floor level.
4. Results
The baseline model predicted annual energy consumption within 15.3% of measured values, consistent with typical ranges reported in the literature. Performance varied significantly by season, with winter predictions deviating by up to 22% while summer predictions remained within 8%.
The simulation results diverged significantly from our experimental measurements at the second-floor transition zone between commercial and residential spaces. Initially I assumed this was an instrumentation error, but after rechecking the calibration data, I realized the discrepancy pointed to a flaw in my thermal model itself. I had been treating the thermal load as uniform across the surface when the actual distribution follows a gradient that shifts with boundary conditions. Going back to re-derive the governing equations with spatially varying boundary conditions produced results within 3% of experimental values — and more importantly, it changed my understanding of why the original model was wrong, not just that it was wrong.

Concept: Three natural moments for guided reflection, each capturing different kinds of evidence. The during-creation reflection catches raw, honest, in-the-moment thinking. Post-completion captures synthesized judgment. Post-feedback captures how students process external evaluation. Together they create a complete evidence loop for every artifact.
During Creation
In-the-moment reflection
Triggered: When Tori detects friction, surprise, or a decision point in what the student is writing
Captures thinking before it gets cleaned up. The student reveals doubts, wrong turns, emotional reactions, and real-time reasoning that never appear in the polished artifact. This is the rawest, most honest evidence.
Unique evidence it captures:
  • First reactions before rationalization
  • Emotional attachment to ideas
  • Decision points and why one path was chosen
  • Connections to past experiences (in real time)
  • The gap between what they know and what they write
After Completion
Synthesized reflection
Triggered: Within 1 hour of submission, before faculty feedback
Captures the student's own assessment of the whole artifact — what worked, what didn't, what they'd do differently. This reveals self-assessment calibration and the ability to step back from their own work.
Unique evidence it captures:
  • Self-assessment accuracy (vs. later faculty feedback)
  • Process reflection (methodology, not just outcomes)
  • Forward-looking intentions for future work
  • Awareness of strengths and weaknesses
  • Principles derived from experience
After Feedback
Evaluative reflection
Triggered: After faculty feedback or grade is posted
Captures how the student processes external evaluation. Do they agree? Are they surprised? Can they integrate feedback with their own self-assessment? This is where growth plans are formed.
Unique evidence it captures:
  • Self-assessment calibration (prediction vs. reality)
  • Ability to learn from criticism
  • Emotional regulation around evaluation
  • Specific growth commitments
  • Attribution of success/failure (internal vs. external)
