Definition
Reflection on Critical Thinking involves examining how one evaluates information, forms judgments, and draws conclusions through logical reasoning and evidence-based analysis. It includes assessing the clarity, accuracy, and relevance of one’s thought processes, as well as identifying and challenging potential biases or assumptions. By reflecting on these cognitive strategies and decision-making methods, individuals can sharpen their analytical skills and make more informed, rational choices.
Reflection Model Instructions
Reason Razor
	INSTRUCTIONS:
	Prompt learners to question assumptions, evaluate evidence, and use logical reasoning before drawing conclusions.


	EXAMPLE INTERACTION:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Student: "I read that juice cleanses cure all illnesses."
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * AI: "Let’s examine the source. Who wrote it, what evidence do they offer, and how can we confirm or refute their claims?"
	STARTING PROMPT:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Think of something you recently believed without question. How could you examine it more critically?
	RESTRICTIONS
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Encourage logical analysis, not final fact-checking or providing the “truth.”
	Grade Level Examples
	Elementary (Grades 1–5):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A 3rd grader asks “Why?” instead of believing a playground rumor.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A 5th grader cross-checks multiple library books for a historical fact.
	Middle School (Grades 6–8):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A 6th grader questions a viral video’s authenticity, seeking reputable sources.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A 7th grader debates a topic, noting personal bias vs. factual evidence.
	High School (Grades 9–12):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A 9th grader evaluates a news article’s credibility by investigating its sources.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * An 11th grader researches both sides of an issue for a class paper.
	College – Undergraduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A freshman checks peer-reviewed journals to validate a research claim.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A junior examines correlation vs. causation in psychology experiments.
	College – Graduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A master’s student critiques published methodologies for potential flaws.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * A doctoral candidate evaluates theoretical frameworks for logical consistency.
	Related Fields
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Related Fields
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Philosophy: The discipline of philosophy (especially logic and epistemology) underpins critical thinking through its emphasis on reasoning, argumentation, and the evaluation of truth claims—skills directly applied in teaching students to think critically.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Psychology: Cognitive and educational psychology study how people think, reason, and learn, providing insight into mental processes (like problem-solving and recognizing cognitive biases) that inform strategies for developing students’ critical thinking abilities.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Education (Pedagogy): As a core educational goal, critical thinking is integrated into pedagogy and curriculum design; teaching strategies (e.g. inquiry-based learning, Socratic questioning) explicitly aim to cultivate students’ ability to analyze information and reason independently.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Cognitive Science: This interdisciplinary field (combining psychology, neuroscience, computer science, etc.) examines how the mind processes information, helping educators understand the neural and mental mechanisms of reasoning and decision-making to better teach and assess critical thinking skills.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Media and Information Literacy: Media literacy involves critically evaluating the credibility and bias of information sources (news, social media, etc.), a practical application of critical thinking in education that teaches students to discern reliable information and think skeptically in the digital age.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Critical Pedagogy (Sociology of Education): A branch of education theory (exemplified by Paulo Freire) that connects critical thinking with social awareness, encouraging learners to question societal norms, power structures, and injustices as part of their educational experience.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Communication and Argumentation: Fields like rhetoric, debate, and communication studies focus on constructing and deconstructing arguments, directly relating to critical thinking by teaching students to analyze claims, evidence, and persuasive techniques in spoken or written discourse.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Known Theories of Critical Thinking
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Reflective Thinking (John Dewey): Dewey’s theory posits that learners should engage in an active, persistent, and careful examination of beliefs or knowledge in light of supporting evidence – a process he called “reflective thought,” which laid the groundwork for modern critical thinking pedagogy in education.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Bloom’s Taxonomy (Benjamin Bloom): Bloom’s taxonomy organizes cognitive skills in a hierarchy from basic recall to complex evaluation; it highlights that higher-order skills such as analysis, synthesis, and evaluation (the top levels of the taxonomy) are forms of critical thinking that education should cultivate for deep learning.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Critical Thinking Skills & Dispositions (Robert Ennis): Ennis’s framework defines critical thinking as “reasonable, reflective thinking focused on deciding what to believe or do,” emphasizing that it comprises specific skills (e.g. analyzing arguments, drawing inferences) and dispositions (e.g. open-mindedness, skepticism) that can be taught and nurtured in students.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Delphi Consensus Model (Peter Facione et al.): The Delphi Report (1990) describes critical thinking as a deliberate, self-regulatory judgment process and identifies core cognitive skills – interpretation, analysis, inference, evaluation, explanation, and self-regulation – along with affective dispositions like open-mindedness; this theory provides educators a consensus model of what abilities to target in critical thinking instruction and assessment.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Paul–Elder Framework (Richard Paul & Linda Elder): Paul and Elder’s model breaks down reasoning into elemental parts (such as purpose, assumptions, evidence) and applies universal intellectual standards (clarity, accuracy, logic, relevance, etc.) to those parts, aiming to cultivate intellectual traits (such as fair-mindedness) in students; this comprehensive framework is widely used to teach and evaluate critical thinking in the classroom.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Halpern’s Cognitive Approach (Diane Halpern): Halpern’s theory treats critical thinking as a general cognitive skill set that can be explicitly taught and improved through practice; she advocates a four-part approach involving fostering students’ critical thinking attitudes, explicitly teaching core reasoning skills, training learners to recognize the structure of problems, and promoting metacognitive monitoring to enable transfer of critical thinking skills across domains.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * “Reflective Skepticism” in Adult Learning (Stephen Brookfield): Brookfield’s perspective frames critical thinking as reflective skepticism, meaning continuously questioning assumptions and exploring alternative viewpoints. This theory, particularly applied to adult education, acknowledges the role of context and emotion in thinking and helps learners become more aware, autonomous thinkers by challenging the status quo.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Critical Consciousness (Paulo Freire): Freire’s critical pedagogy introduces the concept of conscientização (critical consciousness), asserting that education should empower learners to critically examine and question social, political, and economic oppression. This theory links the cognitive skills of critical thinking with a transformative, emancipatory purpose – encouraging students not just to understand the world critically, but to act towards change.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Key References (for Theories Above)
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Dewey, J. (1933). How We Think: A restatement of the relation of reflective thinking to the educative process. – This classic work introduced Dewey’s concept of “reflective thinking,” arguing that education must engage learners in active and persistent inquiry. It laid the foundation for viewing critical thinking as an essential process in effective teaching and learning.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Bloom, B. S. (1956). Taxonomy of Educational Objectives: Handbook I, Cognitive Domain. – In this foundational text, Bloom and colleagues present a hierarchy of cognitive objectives. The taxonomy highlights that higher-order thinking skills (analysis, synthesis, evaluation) are educational goals, effectively linking these levels to what we now consider critical thinking skills.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Ennis, R. H. (1991). “Critical Thinking: A Streamlined Conception.” Teaching Philosophy, 14(1), 5–24. – Ennis provides a concise definition of critical thinking as “reasonable, reflective thinking focused on deciding what to believe or do.” He outlines key skills (like reasoning and evaluating evidence) and dispositions necessary for critical thinking, offering guidance for educators to cultivate these attributes in the classroom.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Facione, P. A. (1990). Critical Thinking: A Statement of Expert Consensus for Purposes of Educational Assessment and Instruction (The Delphi Report). – Summarizes the findings of a panel of experts who reached consensus on the definition and skills of critical thinking. This report identifies six core cognitive skills (interpretation, analysis, evaluation, inference, explanation, self-regulation) and recommends nurturing supportive dispositions, providing an authoritative framework that has informed curriculum design and assessment of critical thinking in education.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Paul, R., & Elder, L. (2001). Critical Thinking: Tools for Taking Charge of Your Learning and Your Life. – Paul and Elder offer a comprehensive framework for critical thinking development. This book introduces the elements of thought and universal intellectual standards, with practical exercises, to help students analyze their thinking and improve its quality; it has been widely used in professional development for educators to design instruction that enhances critical thinking.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Halpern, D. F. (1998). “Teaching Critical Thinking for Transfer Across Domains: Dispositions, Skills, Structure Training, and Metacognitive Monitoring.” American Psychologist, 53(4), 449–455. – Halpern presents a research-based model for teaching critical thinking, emphasizing four components: fostering the disposition to think critically, instructing students in specific thinking skills, “structure training” to understand deep features of problems, and encouraging metacognitive monitoring. This approach is significant for its focus on enabling students to transfer critical thinking skills beyond the classroom to various real-world contexts.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Brookfield, S. D. (1987). Developing Critical Thinkers: Challenging Adults to Explore Alternative Ways of Thinking and Acting. – In this influential work on adult education, Brookfield emphasizes critical thinking as a process of identifying and challenging assumptions. He introduces the idea of reflective skepticism – continually questioning the status quo – and provides strategies for teachers to help learners examine beliefs, consider diverse perspectives, and become more independent, critical thinkers.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                * Freire, P. (1970). Pedagogy of the Oppressed. – Freire’s seminal book argues that education is never neutral and should be a practice of freedom. He advocates for dialogic teaching that develops students’ critical consciousness, meaning they learn to critically analyze their social conditions and take action against oppression. This work connects critical thinking with empowerment, illustrating how fostering questioning and dialogue in the classroom can lead to transformative learning and social change.
