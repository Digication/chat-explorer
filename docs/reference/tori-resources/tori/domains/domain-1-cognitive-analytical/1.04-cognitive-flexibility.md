Reflection on Cognitive Flexibility involves examining one's ability to adapt thoughts and strategies when faced with changing circumstances, new information, or unexpected obstacles. It includes recognizing the mental processes that enable shifting between different ideas, perspectives, or problem-solving methods, and assessing how well one navigates these changes. By reflecting on the mechanisms of cognitive flexibility, individuals can identify barriers to adaptability and develop strategies to become more agile, open-minded, and effective in dynamic environments.

Reflection Model Instructions
FlexiThink
	INSTRUCTIONS:
	Encourage learners to switch strategies or perspectives when circumstances change, fostering adaptable thinking.
	EXAMPLE INTERACTION:
	                                                                                                                                                                           * Student: "I always use the same math approach, and now I’m stuck."
                                                                                                                                                                           * AI: "Trying a different method—like drawing a diagram—may help. Flexibility opens new solutions."
	STARTING PROMPT:
	                                                                                                                                                                           * Recall a situation where your usual strategy wasn’t working. How did you adapt or could you adapt next time?
	RESTRICTIONS
	                                                                                                                                                                           * Prompt adaptive mindset, not direct academic solutions.
	Grade Level Examples
	Elementary (Grades 1–5):
	                                                                                                                                                                           * A 2nd grader changes puzzle-solving tactics instead of repeating the same approach.
                                                                                                                                                                           * A 5th grader switches from crayons to markers to improve clarity on a map.
	Middle School (Grades 6–8):
	                                                                                                                                                                           * A 6th grader tries flashcards instead of rereading notes for memorization.
                                                                                                                                                                           * A 7th grader modifies debate stance mid-discussion after hearing the opponent’s perspective.
	High School (Grades 9–12):
	                                                                                                                                                                           * A 9th grader adjusts from a rigid schedule to a more flexible one to reduce stress.
                                                                                                                                                                           * An 11th grader revises an essay outline after teacher feedback reveals structural issues.
	College – Undergraduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                           * A sophomore pivots research questions when original data is inconclusive.
                                                                                                                                                                           * A senior widens a job search beyond a single industry path.
	College – Graduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                           * A master’s student revises thesis direction upon encountering unexpected data.
                                                                                                                                                                           * A doctoral candidate tests alternative statistical tools when the primary model fails.
	Related Fields
                                                                                                                                                                           * Neuroscience (Executive Functions): Studies brain regions like the prefrontal cortex involved in switching tasks or strategies.

                                                                                                                                                                           * Developmental Psychology: Investigates how flexibility emerges across childhood and adolescence.

                                                                                                                                                                           * Educational Psychology: Explores teaching methods that foster adaptive learning and mental set shifting.

Known Theories
a) Cognitive Flexibility Theory (Spiro, Coulson, Feltovich)
                                                                                                                                                                              * Short Description: Emphasizes the adaptive reconfiguration of mental processes when confronted with changing demands, highlighting the role of context and prior knowledge.

Key References
                                                                                                                                                                                 1. Diamond, A. (2013). “Executive functions.” Annual Review of Psychology, 64.

                                                                                                                                                                                    * Summary: Reviews core executive functions, including cognitive flexibility, and their developmental trajectories.

                                                                                                                                                                                       2. Martin, M. M., & Rubin, R. B. (1995). “A new measure of cognitive flexibility.” Psychological Reports, 76(2).

                                                                                                                                                                                          * Summary: Introduces a standardized measure for assessing individual differences in cognitive flexibility.

                                                                                                                                                                                             3. Spiro, R. J., Coulson, R. L., Feltovich, P. J., & Anderson, D. K. (1988). “Cognitive flexibility theory: Advanced knowledge acquisition in ill-structured domains.” Technical Report, University of Illinois.

                                                                                                                                                                                                * Summary: Foundational text in educational psychology connecting flexible thinking with complex learning domains.

b) Task-Switching Paradigm (Jersild, Rogers, Monsell)
                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Short Description: Focuses on the mental processes required to shift between tasks, investigating how quickly and efficiently individuals can adapt to new rules or goals.

Key References
                                                                                                                                                                                                                  1.                                                                                                                                                                                                                      * Summary: Comprehensive review of cognitive control mechanisms underlying task-switching.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                        2. Monsell, S. (2003). “Task switching.” Trends in Cognitive Sciences, 7(3).

                                                                                                                                                                                                                           * Summary: Seminal review detailing the cost of switching tasks and implications for flexibility.
