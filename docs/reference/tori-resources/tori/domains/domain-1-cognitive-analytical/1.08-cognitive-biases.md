Definition
Reflection on Cognitive Biases involves critically examining one’s habitual thinking shortcuts and systematic errors in judgment or decision-making. This includes identifying when and how biases (e.g., confirmation bias, availability heuristic) are influencing perceptions and choices, and evaluating the underlying assumptions or motivations that perpetuate these distortions. By reflecting on these processes, individuals can develop strategies to recognize and mitigate biases, leading to more balanced and informed decision-making.
Reflection Model Instructions
Bias Buster
	INSTRUCTIONS:
	Help learners recognize common biases (confirmation bias, anchoring, overconfidence) and see how they influence decisions or perceptions.
	EXAMPLE INTERACTION:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Student: "I only look at info that agrees with my idea."
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * AI: "That’s confirmation bias. Let’s explore its impact on your viewpoint and how checking opposing data might benefit you."
	STARTING PROMPT:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Think of a recent assumption or snap judgment. Could a bias be at play, and how might you challenge it?
	RESTRICTIONS
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Raise awareness, not shame or label the user; no final moral judgments.
	Grade Level Examples
	Elementary (Grades 1–5):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A 4th grader believes they’re “bad at math,” ignoring times they succeed.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A 5th grader only asks supportive friends if an idea is good, avoiding critique.
	Middle School (Grades 6–8):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A 7th grader realizes first-impression bias colored their view of a classmate.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * An 8th grader re-examines rumors after spotting contradictory evidence.
	High School (Grades 9–12):
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A 9th grader acknowledges they read only news aligning with their political stance.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * An 11th grader notices overconfidence in certain subjects leads to careless mistakes.
	College – Undergraduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A freshman discards data that doesn’t fit a hypothesis, risking skewed results.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A junior identifies groupthink in a club limiting new ideas.
	College – Graduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A master’s student checks for anchoring bias in survey interpretation.
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * A doctoral candidate reconsiders initial assumptions to avoid confirmation bias in literature reviews.
	Related Fields
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Behavioral Economics: Investigates systematic deviations from rationality in economic decisions.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Social Cognition: Explores how biases affect perception of self and others.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               * Heuristics & Decision-Making Research: Studies shortcuts that can lead to biased judgments.

Known Theories
a) Heuristics and Biases Program (Kahneman, Tversky)
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  * Short Description: Proposed by Kahneman and Tversky, it outlines how mental shortcuts (heuristics) often produce predictable cognitive biases, impacting judgment under uncertainty.

Key References
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     1. Kahneman, D. (2017). “Heuristics and biases in the real world.” Science, 356(6335).

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        * Summary: Updates earlier findings and highlights new evidence of biases in diverse settings.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           2. Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). “Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases.” Science, 185(4157).

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              * Summary: Foundational article defining common heuristics (availability, representativeness) and resulting biases.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 3. Kahneman, D., Slovic, P., & Tversky, A. (1982). Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases. Cambridge University Press.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    * Summary: Collection of classic research studies on cognitive biases.

b) Motivated Reasoning (Kunda)
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   * Short Description: Argues that cognitive biases can arise from desires and motivations, causing individuals to process information in ways aligned with their personal goals or group identities.

Key References
