4.13 Reflection on Reflection (Meta-Reflection)
Definition
Reflection on Reflection (Meta-Reflection) entails evaluating how one engages in reflective practice itself—scrutinizing its depth, accuracy, biases, and effectiveness. By improving the quality of one’s reflective processes, individuals enhance learning, self-awareness, and professional or personal growth outcomes.
Reflection Model Instructions
Reflect on Reflection
	INSTRUCTIONS:
	Prompt learners to consider how reflecting itself affects their awareness, decisions, or learning processes.
	EXAMPLE INTERACTION:
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * Student: "I’m not sure reflection changes anything. Feels like overthinking."
                                                                                                                                                                                    * AI: "Reflection can reveal patterns or highlight growth. Have you noticed any insights after journaling or re-reading past entries?"
	STARTING PROMPT:
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * When you look back on any reflective writing or past thoughts, how has that process impacted your decisions or learning?
	RESTRICTIONS
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * No therapy or counseling instructions; purely about reflection’s benefits.
	Grade Level Examples
	Elementary (Grades 1–5):
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * A 5th grader journals daily, realizing they remember events more clearly.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * A 4th grader reflects on math mistakes, helping them avoid the same errors next time.
	Middle School (Grades 6–8):
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * A 6th grader notices journaling about arguments helps them see both sides calmly.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * An 8th grader reflects on art projects, spotting ways to improve creativity.
	High School (Grades 9–12):
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * A 10th grader does weekly reflections, noticing study patterns that work best.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * An 11th grader sees how thinking about social interactions leads to better friendship management.
	College – Undergraduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * A freshman sets a monthly reflection routine, adjusting academic or social strategies.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * A junior notes leadership improvements by reviewing experiences in clubs or internships.
	College – Graduate:
	                                                                                                                                                                                    * A master’s student systematically examines progress each semester, refining goals.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * A doctoral candidate keeps a research journal, noticing clarity in direction and methods over time.
	

Related Fields
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Metacognition (Cognitive Psychology): Studies “thinking about thinking,” which includes monitoring and regulating one’s own cognitive strategies.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Professional Education (Teacher, Medical Training): Uses guided reflective practice, encouraging practitioners to reflect on their reflections for deeper learning.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Philosophy (Epistemology): Explores self-knowledge limits, biases, and the nature of introspection.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Mindfulness & Contemplative Traditions: Emphasizes awareness of mental processes, noticing and letting go of unhelpful thought patterns.
Known Theories
(Classic) The Reflective Practitioner (Donald Schön)
Short Description:
 Schön distinguished reflection-in-action (thinking on your feet) from reflection-on-action (thinking after the event). Meta-reflection extends this to examining one’s methods of reflection for continuous improvement.
Top 10 Most Cited Peer-Reviewed Articles (Newest to Oldest)
                                                                                                                                                                                    1. Schön, D. A. (1983). The reflective practitioner: How professionals think in action. Basic Books.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Foundational text introducing reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action in professional contexts.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Link (Publisher)
                                                                                                                                                                                    2. Boud, D., Keogh, R., & Walker, D. (1985). Reflection: Turning experience into learning. Kogan Page.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Outlines a model of reflection that includes attending to feelings and re-evaluating experience.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Reference (Taylor & Francis/Kogan Page)
(Emerging) Transformative Learning & Reflexivity (Mezirow)
Short Description:
 Mezirow’s theory posits that reflection can become transformative when it questions underlying assumptions (premise reflection). Reflexivity integrates awareness of personal, cultural, and social influences shaping one’s reflections.
Top 10 Most Cited Peer-Reviewed Articles (Newest to Oldest)
                                                                                                                                                                                    1. Mezirow, J. (1991). Transformative dimensions of adult learning. Jossey-Bass.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Introduces premise reflection and perspective transformation as deeper levels of learning.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Link (Wiley)
                                                                                                                                                                                    2. Mezirow, J. (2000). Learning as transformation: Critical perspectives on a theory in progress. Jossey-Bass.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Collection of essays expanding on critical reflection and adult development.
                                                                                                                                                                                    * Reference (Wiley/Jossey-Bass)
