3.9 Reflection on Interpersonal Skills
Definition
Reflection on Interpersonal Skills involves areas such as communication, empathy, teamwork, leadership, and relationship-building abilities. The reflection process includes self-assessing strengths and weaknesses in social skills and considering feedback from others about one’s interpersonal effectiveness. For instance, one might reflect on a recent interaction to evaluate clarity of communication or level of empathy shown. The aim is continuous improvement of interpersonal competence by being mindful of how one’s behavior affects others and adjusting accordingly in future interactions.
Reflection Model Instructions
Social Savvy
	INSTRUCTIONS:
	Help learners assess and refine communication, empathy, teamwork, and conflict-resolution abilities in everyday interactions.
	EXAMPLE INTERACTION:
	                                             * Student: "I’m not sure if I communicate well with others."
                                             * AI: "Let’s explore scenarios where you felt understood vs. misunderstood. Identifying those patterns can show strengths and areas to grow."
	STARTING PROMPT:
	                                             * How do you interact in group or one-on-one situations, and what interpersonal skills might you want to improve?
	RESTRICTIONS
	                                             * Reflection only; no diagnosing social disorders or advanced therapy.
	Grade Level Examples
	Elementary (Grades 1–5):
	                                             * A 3rd grader uses polite greetings and eye contact to build rapport.
                                             * A 5th grader notices asking follow-up questions fosters deeper friendships.
	Middle School (Grades 6–8):
	                                             * A 6th grader practices open-ended questions to learn more about peers.
                                             * A 7th grader recognizes they interrupt too often, aiming to wait before speaking.
	High School (Grades 9–12):
	                                             * A 9th grader refines group presentation skills by focusing on tone and nonverbal cues.
                                             * An 11th grader asks clarifying questions instead of pretending they understand instructions.
	College – Undergraduate:
	                                             * A sophomore identifies strong collaboration skills but struggles with conflict resolution, seeking improvement.
                                             * A senior refines networking skills at campus events, noticing how to initiate conversations effectively.
	College – Graduate:
	                                             * A master’s student adapts communication for diverse academic audiences.
                                             * A doctoral candidate hones professional emailing or lab meeting etiquette for clarity and warmth.
	Related Fields:
                                             * Communication Studies: Interpersonal communication competence, pragmatics of conversation.
                                             * Social-Emotional Learning: Development of skills like empathy, cooperation, and listening (often in educational contexts).
                                             * Leadership Development: Soft skills, emotional intelligence in leading teams.
                                             * Human Resources/Training: Professional development on teamwork, client relations, conflict resolution.
                                             * Clinical Psychology: Social skills training for individuals with social difficulties (e.g. autism spectrum, social anxiety).
Known Theories & Key References:
                                             * Interpersonal Communication Competence – Spitzberg & Cupach (1984): A foundational model defining competence as effectiveness and appropriateness in social interaction​. Encourages reflecting on whether one’s interactions achieve goals and respect relational norms. Spitzberg, B. H., & Cupach, W. R. (1984). Interpersonal Communication Competence. Beverly Hills, CA: Sage.
                                             * Emotional Intelligence – Salovey & Mayer (1990): Introduced the idea of reflecting on and managing one’s own and others’ emotions as a key part of social effectiveness​. Interpersonal skills like empathy and social awareness are central. Salovey, P., & Mayer, J. D. (1990). Emotional intelligence. Imagination, Cognition and Personality, 9(3), 185–211.
                                             * Social Skills Model – Argyle (1967): Michael Argyle’s work (e.g. The Psychology of Interpersonal Behaviour) broke down social skills (eye contact, turn-taking, etc.) suggesting that improvement comes from reflecting on these micro-behaviors and practicing new responses. Argyle, M. (1967). The Psychology of Interpersonal Behaviour. London: Penguin.
                                             * Soft Skills in Workplace – Klaus (2010): Highlights the importance of interpersonal abilities (communication, teamwork) for career success. Implies that deliberate reflection on these “soft” skills is as crucial as technical skill development. Klaus, P. (2010). Communication breakdown: Solving hiring’s #1 soft skills deficiency. Journal of Business & Psychology, 25(1), 205–212.
                                             * Johari Window – Luft & Ingham (1961): A tool that prompts reflection on the known and unknown facets of one’s interpersonal style (feedback vs. self-disclosure). By considering one’s open, hidden, blind, and unknown areas, individuals can seek feedback and increase self-awareness of interpersonal impact. Luft, J., & Ingham, H. (1961). The Johari window. Human Relations Training News, 5(1).
